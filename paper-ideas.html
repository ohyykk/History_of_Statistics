Paper Ideas

Exploring potential research directions in the history of statistics

Current Research Interests

Throughout my studies in the history of statistics, several research themes have emerged that I find particularly compelling. These ideas represent potential directions for future academic work and deeper exploration.

1. Reviving and Evaluating Historical Visualizations with Modern Tools

Weeks involved: Week 7 (Data Visualization), Week 6 (Institutionalization of Classical Statistics)

Research Question: Can historical visual methods be quantitatively evaluated against modern ones for interpretability and accuracy?

Research Approach:

  • Recreate historical visualization types (e.g., polar area diagrams, flow maps, proportional symbols) using Python (Matplotlib, Seaborn) or R (ggplot2)
  • Compare them empirically with modern equivalents (e.g., heatmaps, network graphs, Sankey diagrams)
  • Run small user studies or algorithmic measures (e.g., information density, chart junk ratio, cognitive load metrics) to assess their performance

Project Idea: Create a reproducible pipeline that visualizes historical and modern versions side-by-side, then analyzes when and why older forms may outperform or underperform. Optionally, share the findings in a digital exhibition or interactive notebook.

2. Bias in Data Representation: A Computational Framework

Weeks involved: Week 7 (Data Visualization), Week 11 (Overlooked Contributors), Week 12 (Statistics and Society)

Research Question: Can we build a tool to detect and visualize bias or distortion in historical and modern statistical graphics?

Research Approach:

  • Define visual biases (e.g., misleading y-axis, selective binning, omitted variables, population-level masking)
  • Use data visualization libraries (e.g., matplotlib, ggplot2, or altair) and statistical tests to flag these biases in visual outputs
  • Optionally, integrate fairness audits or missingness detection

Project Idea: Design a script or dashboard that takes in a dataset and flags risky or misleading visualization choices. This can be applied both to historical graphs and modern data visualizations, showing the evolution (or persistence) of representational bias.

3. Exploring Causal Graphs Through Visual Encodings

Weeks involved: Week 7 (Data Visualization), Week 9 (Causal Inference)

Research Question: How do different visual encodings of causal relationships affect understanding and reasoning in statistics?

Research Approach:

  • Generate variants of causal diagrams (e.g., DAGs) using different visual languages: traditional node-link, annotated graphs, matrices, or interactive forms
  • Evaluate using criteria such as user accuracy, interpretability, or visual complexity
  • Tools might include dagitty, graphviz, or d3.js

Project Idea: Run a small cognitive experiment or survey on how participants interpret different causal graph styles. Analyze results to determine which visual forms best support causal inference for students or practitioners.

4. Machine-Learning-Driven Historical Visualization Classification

Weeks involved: Week 7 (Data Visualization), Week 6 (Classical Statistics and Its Institutions)

Research Question: Can we use machine learning to classify and organize historical statistical graphics by visual form and function?

Research Approach:

  • Collect a digitized set of historical graphics from archives or books (e.g., Friendly and Wainer 2021, Minard collections)
  • Use image classification (e.g., convolutional neural networks) or clustering to group visualizations by type or purpose
  • Optionally annotate with metadata: creator, dataset, encoding method, historical context

Project Idea: Build a small visual database or prototype tool that uses machine learning to identify and categorize historical graphics. Could serve as a research aid for digital historians or a teaching tool on the evolution of visual reasoning.

Methodological Considerations

For any of these research directions, I would approach the work through:

  • Primary source analysis of original statistical papers and correspondence
  • Historical contextualization of statistical developments within their social and political environments
  • Comparative analysis across different statistical traditions and schools of thought
  • Critical examination of how statistical methods have been applied and interpreted over time

These ideas represent starting points for deeper investigation into the rich and complex history of statistical thinking. Each topic offers opportunities to connect historical developments with contemporary challenges in statistics and data science.
