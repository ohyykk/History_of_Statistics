About the Course

STA496 is a self-directed reading course offered through the Department of Statistical Sciences (DoSS) and the Faculty of Information. It explores the development of statistical thinking and the data sciences through a historical lens. Rather than treating statistical methods as static tools, this course investigates why, when, and how foundational ideas such as least squares, regression, and Bayesian inference emerged and the contexts in which they were applied.

We examine both the major contributions of statistics and the more troubling parts of its history, including how some methods were originally developed to support harmful ideas like eugenics. Through weekly readings and reflections, the course encourages me to critically engage with the legacy of the discipline, tracing its roots from early probability theory and astronomical observations to modern debates in causal inference and data science.

The goal is to build a deeper, more thoughtful understanding of the statistics - one that recognizes both the mathematical creativity and the social impact of the methods we use today.

My Focus

Throughout the term, I explored themes including:

  • The early ideas and beliefs behind probability
  • The social and political influences on the rise of statistical practices
  • The evolution of regression and causal inference
  • The overlooked contributions of women and marginalized groups in statistics
  • The divide between traditional statistics and modern data science
